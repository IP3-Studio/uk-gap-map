# REF: essentialTechnologyPost

## Summary
Convergent Research's Gap Map (gap-map.org, v1.0, launched 2025, introduced in the "Essential Technology" blog post) is an interactive database of the R&D bottlenecks holding back science across roughly 20 fundamental fields. Its thesis: humanity could raise its rate of scientific and technological progress by driving resources into ~100 especially catalytic "bridge-scale" projects — mid-scale efforts costing ~$20-50M each that could unlock trillions in downstream value — which existing institutions "can't or won't build." These fall into a category the authors, borrowing Bell Labs language, call "fundamental development": the infrastructure, tools, techniques and technologies that enable entire fields but fit neither the "R" nor the "D" of R&D and are therefore systematically under-funded. Two kinds of constraint create the gaps: physical/natural limits (e.g. light scattering in brain tissue) and institutional/economic ones (how funding is organised). Maps matter, the authors argue, because they make invisible bottlenecks visible and actionable, act as a discovery/matchmaking mechanism connecting motivated researchers, funders and org-builders to unmet needs, and give researchers "permission" for out-of-the-box, deconstructive thinking. The content was built by talking to hundreds of scientists and technologists over several years — surfacing ideas from "brilliant scientists frustrated by systemic barriers" — then formalised into a database by a small team (Christina Agapakis, Murray Cox, Matt Tranzillo) with 30+ acknowledged contributors, and was explicitly modelled on Frontier Climate's Carbon Removal Knowledge Gaps database. The data model is three-tiered: R&D Gaps (bottlenecks) → Foundational Capabilities (the mid-scale infrastructure that would close a gap, ~a few hundred of them, a few dozen deemed most pivotal) → Resources (organisations/FROs/companies/nonprofits, funding programs, papers, roadmaps, whitepapers, technology seeds, and relevant researchers actively working toward each capability). Entries carry risk tags (Safety, Security, Governance/Ethics) to flag dual-use concerns, and capabilities are clustered thematically by the broad gap they address (e.g. "three-dimensional read/write access to intact biological systems" spanning neuroscience, cell biology and molecular engineering). Navigation is by "Filter by Field," category browsing, and an API & Data endpoint; a Contribute form invites new gaps, solutions, efforts and meta-analyses. The authors stress it is deliberately "a crude map of an emerging space — not a prioritization," not comprehensive, and provocative — v1.0 inviting expert correction, with future posts promised on prioritisation, differential technology development and defensive acceleration.

## Structure
- Three-tiered linked data model, not a flat list: (1) R&D Gaps = bottlenecks/problems; (2) Foundational Capabilities = the mid-scale infrastructure/tools that would close a gap; (3) Resources = the concrete real-world efforts attached to each capability. Every entry links up/down this chain, so the map reads as gap -> what would fix it -> who is already doing something about it.
- Scope quantified and bounded: ~20 fundamental fields of science; ~100 headline R&D gaps; a few hundred foundational capabilities (a few dozen flagged as most pivotal); ~10 'bridge-scale' projects. Each field carries ~3-5 pivotal pieces of infrastructure. The bounded, opinionated size is a feature — it signals curation, not an exhaustive index.
- Fields are the primary organising axis and the main filter ('Filter by Field', with All/None toggles). Capabilities are additionally clustered thematically by the broad gap they serve, allowing cross-field meta-themes (e.g. 'three-dimensional read/write access to intact biological systems' spanning neuroscience, cell biology, molecular engineering).
- Resource entries are typed: active entities (FROs, companies, nonprofits, research institutions), funding programs/competitions, scientific literature and reviews, roadmaps and whitepapers, technology prototypes/'seeds', and named researchers/initiators. This turns each gap into an actionable dossier rather than a description.
- Cross-cutting risk tags on entries: Safety Risk, Security Risk, Governance/Ethics Risk — used to flag dual-use or grey-area items while the map otherwise focuses on 'robustly beneficial' advances.
- Framing of causes: each gap is understood through two constraint types — physical/natural limits vs institutional/economic (funding/organisational) neglect — which doubles as an explanation of why the market/state hasn't already closed it.
- Explicitly NOT ranked or prioritised: described as 'a crude map... not a prioritization.' Ordering is by field/theme, not by importance score; prioritisation is deferred to future work.
- Openness and machine-readability: dedicated navigation for R&D Gaps, Capabilities, Resources, About, Contribute, and an 'API & Data' endpoint, so the dataset is queryable and reusable, not just a webpage.
- Community contribution loop: a Contribute form and email invite submissions of new gaps, solutions and efforts, plus suggestions for better presentation and new meta-analyses — the map is positioned as a living v1.0 that improves via expert correction.

## Lessons
- Adopt the three-tier linked model, translated to civic/economic/tech: Gap (the problem/bottleneck) -> Intervention/Capability (the mid-scale thing that would close it: an institution, dataset, standard, piece of infrastructure, policy instrument, market) -> Resources (who is already working on it — orgs, funders, pilots, reports, named people). This is the single most transferable design choice; it makes each gap actionable rather than just descriptive.
- Define your own load-bearing category concept the way they coined 'fundamental development.' For the UK, name the class of thing that falls between markets and the state and is chronically under-provisioned (e.g. 'civic infrastructure', 'enabling capability', 'coordination gap'). A crisp, defensible category is what makes a map an argument rather than a directory.
- Pick a clear primary organising axis and stick to it. They use ~20 scientific fields. A UK map spanning civic, economic and technological gaps needs an equivalent — e.g. domains (housing, health/social care, energy, skills, justice, local government, digital/data infrastructure) — with 'civic / economic / technological' as an orthogonal tag rather than the top-level split, so genuinely cross-cutting gaps (most real ones) aren't forced into one silo.
- Bound the scope and curate hard. ~100 gaps signals judgement and keeps the map legible. Resist the urge to be exhaustive; a curated, opinionated shortlist is more useful and more credible than a giant index, and lets you explicitly flag the 'most pivotal' few dozen.
- Explicitly decide whether you are mapping or prioritising, and say so. They deliberately shipped 'a map, not a prioritization' as v1.0 and deferred ranking. This lowers the bar to launch, invites correction, and avoids premature fights over what matters most — but plan the prioritisation layer (scoring, cost, tractability) as a named future phase.
- Model the *cause* of each gap, not just the gap. Their physical-vs-institutional framing is powerful; a UK version should tag why each gap persists (market failure, misaligned incentives, fragmented responsibility across departments/tiers of government, missing data, regulatory barrier, funding-timescale mismatch). This turns the map into a diagnosis and points to the right kind of fix.
- Attach a cross-cutting risk/consideration tagging system. They use Safety/Security/Governance-Ethics. A civic-economic map should tag e.g. equity/distributional impact, privacy/surveillance, democratic-legitimacy, and regional/'levelling-up' dimensions — so users can filter for and reason about second-order effects, and so the map isn't naively techno-solutionist.
- Type your resources so each gap becomes a dossier. Mirror their resource types with UK-relevant ones: active organisations (charities, think tanks, ALBs, councils, startups), funding sources (UKRI, foundations, devolved-nation funds, local growth funds), evidence (reports, What Works Centre reviews, NAO/Select Committee findings), live pilots, and named people/champions. The value is matchmaking supply of effort to demand.
- Build the contribution loop and treat v1.0 as a conversation-starter. Their map improves through expert submissions and openly asks for correction. Ship an accessible submission form, credit contributors visibly (they name 30+), and frame launch as 'help us fix this' — domain experts correcting a public map is a cheap, high-signal way to improve coverage and build a coalition.
- Make it open and machine-readable from day one — provide an API/downloadable dataset, not just a website. This lets researchers, journalists and other builders run meta-analyses and cross-reference your gaps against spending, geography or outcomes, multiplying the map's reach.
- Steal a proven schema rather than inventing from scratch — they explicitly copied Frontier Climate's Carbon Removal Knowledge Gaps database structure. For a UK map, look at existing evidence-gap-map conventions (e.g. 3ie/Campbell Collaboration EGMs, which use a domain x outcome matrix with colour-coded evidence density) and What Works Centres, and adapt their visual grammar so users already understand how to read it.
- Ground the map in primary field research, not desk aggregation. Their entries came from years of interviews with frustrated practitioners who could name the bottleneck and the missing capability. A UK map's credibility will likewise depend on structured conversations with front-line civic, economic and technical actors, surfaced and attributed, rather than scraping existing lists.
- Lead with an explicit value narrative that justifies why the map exists. They anchor everything to a headline claim ('~100 catalytic projects could raise the rate of progress', '$20-50M unlocking trillions'). A UK map needs an equivalent thesis (e.g. a bounded set of civic/economic capabilities that, if built, would disproportionately unlock growth, resilience or public value) so the map reads as a call to action with a fundable unit of work, not a catalogue of complaints.

# REF: gapMapDataModel

## Summary
Two resources from Convergent Research. (1) The Gap Map (gap-map.org) is an Astro site whose data is a Notion database (entity IDs are Notion page UUIDs); "gaps" are internally called "bottlenecks." It exposes three content types via a top nav — R&D Gaps, Capabilities, Resources — plus Fields as the taxonomy. Current scale: 103 gaps, 369 capabilities, 1,080 resources, 20 fields. The dashboard embeds ALL data client-side (a 2.1MB JSON blob), enabling instant search/sort/filter. A Gap has one Field, many Capabilities (avg 3.8, up to 13), free-text tags, and an editorial urgency rank. A Capability belongs back to 1–2 Gaps and links to many Resources (avg 3.1). A Resource is essentially a titled external URL with a controlled resourceType. Fields are 20 scientific disciplines, each with a description and a color gradient for visual coding. Controls: multi-select Field filter (All/None), Fuse.js fuzzy search (weighted across name/field/tags/capability names), a grid/list view toggle, a tag filter, a "private tag" banner for unpublished content, and a Sort control (rank | name | number). "Sort by rank" orders gaps by bottleneck_rank (an "Urgency: X/5" editorial score, ties broken by bottleneck_number); capabilities/resources sort by type|alpha instead. URLs: /gaps/{slug}, /capabilities/{slug}, /fields/{slug}, with ?sort=rank&... query params. (2) The /ecosystem page (convergentresearch.org) is a separate Next.js + Sanity CMS app — NOT part of the Gap Map data model. It catalogs FROs (Focused Research Organizations) as a Sanity document type `fro`, categorized by a `topic` document type. Six topic filter tabs plus "All": Mathematics, Astrophysics, Neuroscience, Climate, Biology, Artificial Intelligence. 12 FROs listed, each shown as a card with name, slug, a one-line tagline, and a single topic. FRO detail pages carry richer fields: tagline, founded year, location, website, about/mission, core technology platform, "capability targets" (focus areas), leadership (people + roles + advisory board), named funders/supporters, job listings, resources (publications/blog posts), and social links. Key insight for a UK equivalent: the Gap Map's power is a clean 4-entity relational model (Field → Gap → Capability → Resource) with one editorial urgency ranking, shipped as static embedded JSON. Organizations are deliberately kept in a SEPARATE system (the ecosystem/FRO directory) rather than joined into the gap model — orgs appear inside the gap map only indirectly, as Resources tagged type "Company"/"Research Org"/"FRO"/"Initiative"/"Funding Program".

## Structure
- ENTITY: Gap (Notion type 'bottleneck'; 103 records; page /gaps/{slug}). Fields: id (UUID); bottleneck_name (title); bottleneck_description (long rich text, multi-paragraph); slug; bottleneck_rank (int 0-5, editorial 'Urgency /5'); bottleneck_number (stable int ID 1-104); field (single relation to Field); foundational_capabilities (array of Capability, avg 3.8, range 1-13); tags (array of strings); privateTags (array); last_edited_time (ISO8601).
- ENTITY: Capability (Notion type 'foundational capability' / 'fc'; 369 records; page /capabilities/{slug}). Fields: id (UUID); fc_name (title); fc_description (rich text); slug; rank (int); resources (array of Resource, avg 3.1, range 0-22); bottlenecks (back-reference array to parent Gaps, avg 1.1, range 0-2); tags; privateTags; last_edited_time.
- ENTITY: Resource (1,080 records; external references). Fields: id (UUID); resource_title; resource_url (external link, often to papers/companies); resourceTypes (array from controlled vocabulary); last_edited_time. Resources attach to Capabilities, not directly to Gaps.
- ENTITY: Field / discipline taxonomy (20 records; page /fields/{slug}). Fields: id (UUID); field_name; field_description (rich text intro to the discipline); colorName + colorClass (e.g. 'field-gradient-14', for consistent color coding across cards); last_edited_time. Each Gap belongs to exactly one Field.
- CONTROLLED VOCAB resourceTypes (11 values, with counts): Research and Reviews (336), Initiative (195), Whitepapers and Essays (141), Company (134), Technology Seed (104), Funding Program (59), Research Org (58), Roadmap (24), Individual (11), FRO (10), FRO Concept (8). NOTE: organizations/companies/funders live here as Resources, not as a first-class entity.
- CONTROLLED VOCAB tags (cross-cutting, applied to Gaps & Capabilities): Security Risk, Safety Risk, Governance/Ethics Risk, Longevity and Aging, Brain-Body Interactions, Origin of Life, Recycling. (Separate from Fields; used for filtering + fuzzy search.)
- THE 20 FIELDS: Neuroscience, Cellular and Molecular Biology, Physiology and Medicine, Immunology, Metascience, Mechanical Engineering, Synthetic Biology, Astrophysics, Computation, Ecology, Physics, Social Science, Chemistry, Biosecurity, Biophysics, Geophysics and Climate, Global Health, Nanoscale Fabrication, Space Engineering, Materials Science.
- RELATIONSHIPS: Field 1—* Gap (each Gap has one Field, color-inherited). Gap 1—* Capability (foundational_capabilities; a Gap's card shows 'Show N related Capabilities', expandable inline). Capability *—1..2 Gap (back-link array). Capability 1—* Resource. So the join graph is Field → Gap → Capability → Resource, a strict 4-level hierarchy with no direct Gap↔Resource or Gap↔Org edge.
- FILTERS / CONTROLS (Gap Map): (a) 'Filter by Field' multi-select checklist of 20 fields with All/None buttons; (b) free-text Search using Fuse.js fuzzy matching, field weights: name .5, field_name .3, tags .4, foundational_capabilities.fc_name .4 + fc_description, bottleneck_rank .2, bottleneck_number .2; (c) grid/list View toggle; (d) tag filter (selectedTag) and a Private-Tag banner exposing unpublished/preview content via ?privateTag; (e) Sort control.
- SORT-BY-RANK MECHANICS: URL ?sort=rank is the default. For Gaps, 'rank' sorts by bottleneck_rank descending (the 0-5 'Urgency' score; capability cards render 'Urgency: X/5'), ties broken by bottleneck_number ascending. Other sort keys: 'name' (localeCompare alphabetical) and 'number' (bottleneck_number). Per-view options: Gaps = [rank, alpha]; Capabilities = [type, alpha]; Resources = [type, alpha]. Ranking is editorial/manual, curated in the CMS, not computed.
- DELIVERY ARCHITECTURE (Gap Map): Astro SSR + hydrated islands (FieldFilter, Search, SortControl, ViewToggle, DashboardGrid). The ENTIRE dataset (all gaps/capabilities/resources/fields with nested relations) is serialized into the DashboardGrid component props as one ~2MB JSON payload, so all filtering/sorting/search happens client-side with no API. Source of truth = Notion.
- SEPARATE ENTITY: FRO / Organisation (convergentresearch.org/ecosystem — Next.js + Sanity CMS, distinct app). Sanity type 'fro'; page /ecosystem/{slug}. Listing-card fields: name, slug, one-line tagline/description, single topic (category). Detail-page fields: name, tagline, founded year, location, website URL, about/mission (rich text), core technology/platform name, 'capability targets' (array of focus areas), leadership (people with roles: founders, CEO/COO, Scientific Advisory Board), named funders/supporters (array), job listings, resources (publications + blog posts), social links.
- ORG CATEGORY taxonomy (Sanity type 'topic'): fields _id, name, slug.current, _createdAt/_updatedAt. Six topics used as filter tabs (plus 'All'): Mathematics, Astrophysics, Neuroscience, Climate, Biology, Artificial Intelligence. Each FRO is tagged with one topic; the page groups/filters FROs by topic tab + a field-filter dropdown. 12 FROs currently: CHI-FRO, Cultivarium, [C]Worthy, Dragonfly, E11 Bio, Echo Labs, EvE Bio, Forest Neurotech, Imprint, Lean FRO, Meridial, Parallel Squared Technology Institute.
- CROSS-RESOURCE INSIGHT: the Gap Map and the Ecosystem/FRO directory are TWO separate systems on different stacks (Astro+Notion vs Next+Sanity) with DIFFERENT category taxonomies (20 scientific Fields vs 6 broad Topics). Organizations are intentionally NOT modeled as nodes in the gap graph; they surface in the gap map only as typed Resources ('Company','Research Org','FRO','Initiative','Funding Program').

## Lessons
- Adopt the 4-entity hierarchy as your spine: Field (discipline) → Gap → Capability → Resource. It is clean, it renders as expandable cards (Gap card shows count of child Capabilities), and it cleanly separates the problem (Gap) from the proposed solution (Capability) from the evidence (Resource). A UK gap map should reuse exactly this shape.
- Give every Gap a single Field (for color-coded, mutually-exclusive categorization and filtering) PLUS a multi-value free-text tag list (for cross-cutting themes like 'Security Risk', 'Net Zero', 'Sovereign Capability'). One-of taxonomy for color/filter + many-of tags for nuance is the pattern that works.
- Rank is editorial, not algorithmic: store an integer urgency/priority score (0-5) on each Gap, curated in the CMS, and make 'sort by rank' the default view. Break ties with a stable sequential number field. Do not try to compute rank — a hand-curated priority order is the product's core opinion. Display it prominently (e.g. 'Urgency: 4/5').
- Keep two distinct stable identifiers per Gap: a semantic slug for URLs (/gaps/{slug}) and an immutable integer 'number' as the human-facing ID and tie-breaker. Ranks can be re-ordered without changing the number.
- Model Resources as lightweight typed links, not heavyweight records: just title + external URL + a controlled 'resourceType'. A rich resourceType vocabulary (paper, review, company, initiative, funding program, roadmap, whitepaper, org, individual) lets one resource table serve papers, companies AND funders — avoiding premature separate tables.
- Attach Resources to Capabilities, not to Gaps, and let a Capability back-link to its parent Gap(s). This keeps evidence next to the specific proposed solution and keeps Gap descriptions clean/narrative.
- Do NOT fold organisations into the gap data model. Convergent keeps its FRO/org directory in a completely separate app. In the gap map, orgs appear only as typed Resources. For a UK version, a separate 'UK org/ecosystem directory' with its own richer schema (funding, leadership, location, founded year) is the right architecture; link the two loosely via resourceType='Company'/'Org'.
- For an org/ecosystem directory, the useful per-org fields to collect are: name, slug, one-line tagline, single category/topic, website, founded year, location, mission (rich text), focus areas / capability targets (list), leadership (people + roles + advisory board), funders/supporters (list), open roles, and publications — mirror the FRO detail page.
- Use a small broad topic taxonomy (~6 tabs) for the org directory but a larger fine-grained field taxonomy (~20 disciplines) for the gaps. Different granularities serve different audiences; don't force one shared taxonomy across both.
- Ship the whole dataset as embedded static JSON and do all search/sort/filter client-side. At this scale (~100 gaps, ~370 capabilities, ~1000 resources = ~2MB) there is no need for a backend API; a static build from a CMS (Notion/Sanity/Airtable) gives instant UX and trivial hosting.
- Implement search as weighted fuzzy matching (Fuse.js-style) across name, description, field, tags, and child capability names/descriptions — so a query hits a Gap via its solutions too, not just its title.
- Build a 'private tag' / preview mechanism into the schema from day one (a privateTags field + a banner) so draft or embargoed gaps can be shared with reviewers via a URL param before public launch — useful for a governance/consultation workflow.
- Persist all view state in the URL query string (?sort=rank, field filters, tag, search) so specific filtered views are shareable and linkable — important for policy/stakeholder communication.
- Give each Field a color gradient and a descriptive intro paragraph with its own page (/fields/{slug}). The color coding threads visual identity through every card and the field pages double as topic landing pages for SEO and orientation.

# REF: europe2031

## Summary
"Europe 2031 — What getting AI wrong means for us" (europe2031.ai; PDF snapshot 29 June 2026; 68pp) is scenario fiction by eight European AI-policy figures: Daan Juijn (research director, Arq Foundation, Brussels, which funded the site/translations), Stan van Baarsen (Delta Institute; Dutch National AI Plan), Judith Dada (VC general partner; advises German government), Maximilian Negele and Lily Stelling (Oxford Martin AI Governance Initiative), Philip Fox (KIRA Center; International AI Safety Report), Alex Petropoulos (Arq chief of staff), Michiel Bakker (MIT professor; Google DeepMind), edited by Tom Chivers. They claim no AI-industry funding, personal capacities.

Told through fictional Brussels official Caroline Dubois and expat founder Christian Vogt, it runs Jan 2025–Jun 2026 on real events (DeepSeek R1, the largely illusory EUR 200bn InvestAI, Gigafactory delays, a US executive order screening frontier models pre-release), then speculates to 2031 with fictional labs Atlas (US), Helios (EU), Zimo (China). The chain: US compute advantage (17→255 GW vs Europe 1.4→21 GW) compounds via AI-automated R&D; open-weight cyber models unleash ransomware that hits "Buy European" agencies hardest; Washington rations frontier inference (FISR tiers — Five Eyes, Japan, Korea, Netherlands Tier 1; most of the EU Tier 2); the EU's Anti-Coercion Instrument fails a qualified-majority vote; continually-learning agents gut white-collar and graduate hiring; tax bases erode as value flows to US firms via low-tax jurisdictions; France is downgraded, the euro cracks; China buys influence via loans to southern Europe; the US coerces the sale of a distressed German automaker to Atlas; endgame — Washington demands control of ASML, Europe's only chokepoint, while Beijing counter-threatens rare earths and robot licences.

The epilogue (2034 retrospective) carries the recommendations: tens-of-gigawatt Special Compute Zones with three-month permitting, partnering US hyperscalers but anchoring assets under European law; a middle-power coalition (NL, DE, FR, Norway, UK, Canada, Japan, S. Korea) pooling supply-chain leverage; robotics/world-model industrialisation plus investment screening; Danish-style flexicurity; a positive public vision; leverage as the only route to slowing the race.

The UK fares comparatively well: its AI Security Institute is the sole European body in the cyber-defence coalition, the UK is Tier 1 via Five Eyes, and post-Brexit it brokers bilateral US deals — but that status is discretionary, and the economic, fiscal and labour mechanisms fully apply to services-heavy Britain.

Credibility: well-credentialed authors, mechanisms grounded in real precedent (FDPR/Huawei), honest about speculating — but single-scenario advocacy assuming fast timelines, lab-sympathetic framing, and a deregulatory policy lean.

## Structure
- Chronological dated chapters (Jan 2025 - Mar 2031), each headed by month + title, with a recurring quantitative sidebar tracking US vs Europe installed AI compute in GW and the US 'compute advantage' multiple (12.4x -> 9x -> 12.3x) as the single through-line metric.
- Three-part arc with an explicit epistemic status marker: prologue of real, verifiable events using real companies (Jan 2025 - Jun 2026); a flagged 'from here we speculate' scenario (Aug 2026 - Mar 2031) using anonymised composite actors — Atlas (leading US lab), Helios (leading EU lab), Zimo (leading Chinese lab); and a 2034 epilogue.
- Two viewpoint characters bridge worlds — a Brussels DG TRADE official and a German founder in San Francisco — with recurring text-message exchanges used as fast commentary/foreshadowing between narrative sections.
- Entity types woven through: states (US, China, EU27, UK, Japan, Korea), labs, chokepoint firms (ASML, TSMC, NVIDIA), and policy instruments (FISR inference-tiering, US pre-release screening EO, EU Digital Sovereignty Regulation, Anti-Coercion Instrument, Project Glasswing, Frontier AI Initiative).
- Recommendations are not a standalone list; they are embedded as counterfactuals in the epilogue's retrospective AI-conducted interview (Special Compute Zones, middle-power coalition, robotics/world-models, flexicurity, positive vision, contingency plans).
- Published as a live multi-language website (with a markdown version advertised 'for AI agents') plus a generated PDF snapshot; no footnotes or citations — pre-2026 claims are traceable to news events but not referenced.
- Causal ranking implicit in the narrative: compute -> model access -> cyber/economic dependence -> fiscal erosion -> political fragmentation -> loss of the last chokepoint (ASML); each chapter escalates one link of the chain.

## Lessons
- Make compute the master variable: encode UK-jurisdiction AI compute (GW, installed vs announced vs delivered) against US/China trend lines; the report's sidebar shows Europe growing 1.4->21 GW while the gap still widens — a UK map should track relative share and delivery slippage, not announcements.
- Distinguish location from control: datacentres on UK soil owned by US hyperscalers remain commandeerable via US law (the EO and FISR mechanisms); the gap is capacity 'bolted to the floor' — anchored under UK law, contracts, and energy supply.
- Model frontier-model access as a revocable licence: the UK's Tier 1/Five Eyes treatment in the scenario is a discretionary Washington call 'every three months, forever'; encode the absence of guaranteed-access mechanisms (treaty-level or long-term contractual) and a trigger scenario for inference rationing or price shocks.
- Map cyber-defence access below the elite tier: the ransomware wave hits institutions without frontier-class defence — hospitals, councils, universities; AISI access is not NHS access; encode which UK public institutions could obtain frontier cyberdefence when offensive capability proliferates via open weights.
- Weight white-collar services exposure heavily: displacement lands first on law, consulting, finance, software and graduate intake — the UK's economic core; encode sector/region exposure with entry-level hiring as the leading indicator (the report's 'worst graduate market in living memory').
- Encode the fiscal transmission gap: AI value captured by US firms routed through low-tax jurisdictions erodes the tax base exactly as welfare demand and debt-service costs rise; map UK dependence on high-earner PAYE and services corporation tax, and gilt-market headroom.
- Include labour-transition machinery as a named gap: the report's fix is Danish flexicurity — wage insurance, retraining and displaced-worker support paired with firing flexibility to enable deep adoption; the UK has neither the insurance layer nor an adoption strategy tied to it.
- Build a leverage inventory: the UK holds no ASML-class chokepoint, so it has less coercive protection than the EU; its actual levers are talent, AISI evaluation capacity, finance, and diplomacy — map each lever and the missing strategy to use them, including the report's explicitly UK-inclusive middle-power coalition (NL, DE, FR, Norway, Canada, Japan, Korea).
- Cover asset retention: distressed high-tech firms and successful public-programme researchers are acquired the moment they matter (Atlas buying the automaker and poaching Frontier Initiative staff); map NSI Act screening coverage against AI-era assets (robotics, world models, chip IP) and compute-budget-based talent raids.
- Encode institutional cognition as a first-order gap: the report's root failure is governments unable to perceive or act at AI speed — civil servants banned from frontier tools, evidence standards demanding peer review during monthly capability jumps; measure government frontier-tool access, evaluation capacity, and decision latency versus model release cadence.
- Require contingency plans ('Plan B'): the scenario's sovereignty policies assumed success and had no fallback when the domestic champion lagged; encode trigger-based contingencies for access cut-off, key-firm acquisition, and ally rationing — the memo Caroline wrote and Brussels shelved.
- Track legitimacy and benefit-sharing: anti-AI backlash with no positive vision destroys political room in every country in the scenario, while the US buys time with AI-funded cash transfers; map UK mechanisms for distributing AI gains and public-attitude data as a gap in its own right.
- Include energy and permitting concretely: the benchmark is permitting cut from two years to three months, concierge teams, converted decommissioned plants and new generation; encode UK grid-connection queues and planning timelines for AI Growth Zones against that bar (note the scenario has OpenAI abandoning a UK datacentre over 'regulatory hurdles' and Fluidstack leaving London).
- Add a physical-AI dependency axis: China's humanoid-robotics scale becomes a second coercion channel (rare earths, robot export licences); map UK robotics manufacturing capacity and supply-chain exposure separately from cognitive-AI dependence.
- Measure gaps in capability-time, not static rank: Helios stays a constant '18 months behind' while the absolute gap explodes because release cycles compress; a UK gap map should encode refresh cadence and time-to-frontier, and re-score frequently rather than ranking once.

# REF: crMethodology

## Summary
Convergent Research (CR), the nonprofit founded 2021 by Adam Marblestone and Anastasia Gamick that designs/launches Focused Research Organizations (FROs), published the "Fundamental Development Gap Map" v1.0 on 15 April 2025 at gap-map.org. Lead authors: Adam Marblestone, Mary Wang, Joseph Fridman, with 30+ external contributors. It maps "fundamental development" gaps — mid-scale ("bridge-scale") infrastructure, tools, datasets and techniques that whole fields need but individual labs, academia or VC won't build (analogues to telescopes, accelerators, the Human Genome Project).

Scale/thesis: ~20 scientific fields, each with ~3-5 pivotal gaps, implying ~100 bridge-scale projects at ~$20-50M each. The live database holds roughly 101 R&D gaps, ~363 foundational capabilities and ~977 resources.

Sourcing: gaps come from "hundreds of conversations" / interviews with scientists and technologists over several years ("catalytic ideas... from brilliant scientists frustrated by systemic barriers"), plus a "detector-and-predictor" model — open calls/solicitations (~100 applications reviewed), sponsored workshops, and roadmapping fellows. Community input via a Contribute form, gapmap@convergentresearch.org, and social channels. Two structural lenses guide gap-finding: physical/first-principles constraints and the political economy of research institutions.

Prioritisation: deliberately NOT ranked in v1.0 — "a crude map of an emerging space, not a prioritization of where you should go inside it." No scores. They flag future posts on prioritisation frameworks (differential technology development, defensive acceleration). Entries carry three risk tags — Safety, Security, Governance/Ethics — and they "mostly focus on robustly beneficial advancements."

Governance/cadence: thinly specified. It is explicitly community-iterative and editorially owned by CR, but there is no published curator list, review workflow, per-entry provenance, or committed update schedule — a notable weakness. Versioned as v1.0.

Stated limitations (prominent and repeated): "not by any means a comprehensive survey or prioritized roadmap"; "isn't comprehensive - at all"; inevitably incomplete; illustrative of a category rather than exhaustive.

FRO link: gaps are meant to seed coordinated programs, but CR explicitly de-couples the map from FROs — solutions may be "FRO-shaped and otherwise," including ARPA programs, companies, prize competitions and academia. FROs themselves: nonprofit startups, 10-30 FTE, $20-50M, 3-7 years, ~a dozen launched since 2021.

Sources: gap-map.org/about and /resources; essentialtechnology.blog "Introducing the Convergent Research Gap Map"; issues.org FRO field notes (Marblestone/Gamick/Wang/Fridman); MIT News 2025-06-06.

## Structure
- Three-tier linked data model: (1) R&D Gaps (~101) — broad 'fundamental development' bottlenecks; (2) Foundational Capabilities (~363) — the mid-scale tools/tech/data/infrastructure that would close a gap; (3) Resources (~977) — the concrete evidence and actors tied to each capability. Capabilities are 'thematically organized by which broad R&D gap they address', and resources hang off capabilities.
- Top-level partition by scientific field: ~20 fields, each expected to hold ~3-5 pivotal gaps. Users browse by field, by gap, or by keyword, or explore a graph/network view showing the gap-capability-resource links.
- Resource entries are heterogeneous and typed: active entities (FROs, companies, nonprofits, research institutions), funding programs/competitions, scientific literature (reviews, papers), roadmaps, whitepapers/speculative pieces, technology prototypes/proofs-of-concept, and individual researchers/project initiators.
- Risk taxonomy applied as tags across entries: Safety Risk, Security Risk, Governance/Ethics Risk — used to caveat rather than to rank.
- No ranking or scoring layer by design: v1.0 is explicitly descriptive ('crude map... not a prioritization'). No numeric priority, urgency or impact score is exposed, though they signal intent to add 'attributes to help orient users to urgency or potential impact' later.
- Contribution/feedback layer bolted onto the browse model: a Contribute submission form plus email and social channels feed candidate gaps/capabilities/resources, editorially owned by Convergent Research.
- Framing/entry documents: a conceptual blog post ('fundamental development', 'bridge-scale', $20-50M sizing), an About page (contents + how to use), and a Resources page — these carry the methodology and limitations narrative rather than per-entry metadata.
- Versioning: released and labelled as v1.0, positioning the whole resource as an evolving artifact rather than a fixed reference.

## Lessons
- Name an accountable editorial owner and publish the curation workflow up front. The CR map's biggest governance weakness is that it under-specifies who curates, how submissions are vetted, and how conflicts are handled. A UK map should designate a curator/editorial board, publish acceptance criteria, and log contributor identity so the map reads as a neutral reference, not a pipeline for one funder.
- Separate description from prioritisation, and stage them deliberately. CR shipped a descriptive 'crude map, not a prioritization' first and reserved ranking for later. Copy this: launch descriptive, and when you add ranking make the criteria explicit, dated, and visibly subjective rather than letting a score masquerade as objective truth.
- Triangulate gap-sourcing to avoid capture and advocacy. CR combined interviews with two structural lenses — first-principles physical constraints and the political economy of institutions — plus open calls (~100 applications) and workshops. Relying only on enthusiastic expert interviews biases toward well-connected fields; a UK map should cross-check nominations against independent evidence and gap-analysis, and cap self-nomination.
- Version and date everything, with per-entry provenance and a committed refresh cadence. CR labelled the whole map v1.0 but did NOT publish a per-entry 'last reviewed' date or an update schedule — the main staleness risk. A UK map should stamp each entry with source, contributor, date added, and last-verified, and commit publicly to a review cycle (e.g. quarterly or biannual).
- Build in a lifecycle/retirement process for entries tied to live initiatives. Because resources point at real organizations and programs that get funded, completed, or shut down, entries decay into 'stale advocacy' fast. Assign owners to mark initiatives completed/defunct and to demote gaps that have been substantially addressed, so the map tracks reality rather than boosting yesterday's asks.
- Keep contribution frictionless but gated by editorial review. CR's Contribute-form-plus-email model lowers the barrier; pair that openness with a clear review gate and published inclusion standards so the map grows without becoming a noticeboard or a lobbying surface.
- De-couple gaps from any single funding vehicle. CR explicitly says solutions can be 'FRO-shaped and otherwise' — ARPA-style programs, companies, prizes, academia. A UK map should describe gaps mechanism-agnostically; tying every gap to one instrument (or one organisation's programme) is what turns a map into advocacy.
- Use a linked graph data model (gap to capability to resource to field) rather than flat lists. It keeps entries navigable, forces de-duplication, and makes coverage gaps visible — but budget for the maintenance overhead that graph curation demands.
- Tag risks and caveats on entries (safety, security, governance/ethics). CR's three-tag system signals critical distance and counters uncritical techno-optimism; a UK map, especially if public-facing, should carry comparable caveats plus explicit limitations text ('not comprehensive, not a ranked roadmap') to set expectations and invite correction.
- State limitations loudly and repeatedly. CR foregrounds incompleteness ('not by any means a comprehensive survey', 'isn't comprehensive - at all'). Prominent, honest scoping language protects credibility, deflects the 'why isn't X here' critique, and actively recruits corrections — a cheap but powerful anti-staleness mechanism.
