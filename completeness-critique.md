# Completeness critic

## Missing domains (ranked)
### 1. Health and social care (including mental health)
- **Rationale:** The single largest area of UK state activity and public anxiety has zero coverage on the map — the closest entry is a loneliness evidence institution under civic action. It is also rich in exactly the map's signature gap-shapes: the Casey Commission's funding-reform phase does not report until 2028, leaving a multi-year vacuum with no interim instrument; the Fair Pay Agreement machinery for care workers only takes effect around April 2028 via a negotiating body still being stood up; and prevention, dentistry access, and NHS data infrastructure all have concrete missing institutions rather than just 'more money' asks. Omitting health makes the map look like a digital-governance map rather than a society-wide one.
- **Research brief:** Inventory institution- and instrument-shaped gaps (not spending asks) across NHS, adult social care and mental health. Start with: (1) the gap between the Casey Commission's 2026 interim and 2028 final reports — is there any bridging funding or implementation vehicle for social care (see gov.uk terms of reference and the government response to the HSCC 'cost of inaction' report)? (2) the Fair Pay Agreement pipeline (secondary legislation autumn 2026, negotiating body Oct 2026, first FPA April 2028 — gov.uk factsheet Feb 2026) and what workforce infrastructure (registration, career ladder, training body) England still lacks versus Scotland/Wales; (3) NHS secure data environments and the stalled national data-for-research architecture post-GPDPR; (4) missing evidence/delivery institutions for prevention (no ring-fenced prevention budget mechanism), NHS dentistry contract reform, and children's mental health waiting-list accountability. For each, name the specific missing body, fund or uncommenced provision, who could plausibly build it, and cite the current status document.

### 2. Justice, courts and access to justice
- **Rationale:** Several existing domains (surveillance, privacy, debt) end in 'no collective redress route' or 'no enforcement' — and all of those bottom out in a justice system the map never examines. The Crown Court backlog is at record levels (~77–80k cases, trials listed into 2029–30), Leveson's Independent Review Part 1 (July 2025) drew a December 2025 government response whose measures (intermediate court, curtailed jury trials, 130k sitting days) are largely unfunded or unlegislated, legal aid deserts and the post-PACCAR litigation-funding hole persist, and prisons/IPP remain in crisis. This is highly distinct from every current domain and packed with concrete, fillable gaps.
- **Research brief:** Map fillable gaps across criminal and civil justice. Anchor on: (1) which Leveson Part 1 recommendations the December 2025 government response accepted but has not funded or introduced in the Courts and Tribunals Bill (compare the Bar Council tracker and the gov.uk bill factsheet); (2) civil side — no generic opt-out collective redress regime outside competition law (cross-reference the surveillance and privacy domains' redress entries), the unlegislated fix to litigation funding after PACCAR (CJC review recommendations), and legal aid deserts (map provider coverage by area of law using Law Society heatmaps); (3) missing institutions: no criminal justice inspectorate with system-wide (cross-agency) remit, no problem-solving courts rollout vehicle, no independent body for miscarriage-of-justice compensation reform, coroner service fragmentation; (4) prisons: IPP resentencing and capacity contingency. Each entry should specify the instrument (statute, fund, body) that would close it.

### 3. Local-state capacity, devolution and public finance
- **Rationale:** Almost every gap already on the map (community right to buy, youth services, planning reform, co-op development, debt collection standards) assumes a functioning local state — yet local government's own machinery is the map's biggest blind spot. The local audit system collapsed (918 unaudited accounts at peak; the 2024–26 'backstop' cleared the backlog largely via disclaimed opinions, meaning years of accounts with no real assurance), the Local Audit Office is only now being legislated, section 114 notices recur, England has no fiscal devolution to speak of, and there is no serious capability pipeline for council finance, procurement and data roles. This is distinct from the civic-action domain (community-level) and from stagnation (national infrastructure).
- **Research brief:** Document gaps in the machinery of sub-national government in England. Cover: (1) the assurance vacuum left by disclaimer-opinion backstops (gov.uk 'Addressing the local audit backlog' and 'Local audit reform' strategy) — what the new Local Audit Office will and won't fix, and whether any body will re-audit the lost years; (2) absence of fiscal devolution instruments (no local revenue-raising beyond council tax/business rates; compare Manchester/West Midlands trailblazer asks) and no independent local-government fiscal watchdog (an OBR-for-councils); (3) workforce: no national capability programme for council finance officers, procurement and digital (note dedupe: planners are already covered under stagnation); (4) no standing 'failure regime' institution for pre-s114 intervention, and the unfinished Office for Local Government (Oflog) abolition/successor question. Produce entries naming the missing body or statutory instrument and its plausible sponsor (MHCLG, LGA, devolved combined authorities, philanthropy).

### 4. National resilience and civil preparedness (biosecurity, pandemics, climate adaptation, CNI)
- **Rationale:** The map has an 'AI crisis' preparedness domain but nothing for every other tail risk — a striking asymmetry. The Covid-19 Inquiry Module 1 recommended a UK-wide independent statutory body for whole-system emergency preparedness that still does not exist; the July 2025 Resilience Action Plan was judged 'ambitious with room to go further' (CLTR); the UK has no civilian volunteer protection corps on the model of Germany's THW; climate adaptation has no delivery body despite repeated CCC 'not prepared' verdicts; and stockpiles, undersea-cable and energy-CNI resilience all lack owners. These are concrete, buildable institutions — ideal gap-map material.
- **Research brief:** Build the resilience gap inventory from three baselines: the Covid-19 Inquiry recommendations tracker (covid19.public-inquiry.uk) versus the government's Module 1/Module 2 responses and the twice-yearly implementation updates starting November 2026; the UK Government Resilience Action Plan (July 2025) versus CLTR's critique; and the CCC adaptation progress reports. Extract every accepted-but-unbuilt institution (the independent statutory preparedness body, a standing scientific advisory mechanism outside emergencies, a national volunteer/civil-protection corps and the DCMS civil society response protocol's limits), every unfunded capability (stockpile strategy, surge manufacturing, Exercise Pegasus follow-through), and adaptation delivery gaps (no body owns overheating homes, surface-water flooding, or a national adaptation finance mechanism). Include biosecurity: status of the Biological Security Strategy's funding and the promised real-time pathogen surveillance network. Note explicitly where entries border the stagnation domain's energy items to avoid duplication.

### 5. Housing delivery, safety and tenure
- **Rationale:** Housing is the UK's most salient economic dysfunction and the current map only touches it obliquely via planning (stagnation) and community assets (civic action). A dedicated domain is justified because housing is dense with the map's favourite pattern — legislated-but-switched-off: the Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 remains largely uncommenced with core valuation reforms drifting to 2027–28, commonhold awaits a 2026–27 bill, building-safety remediation post-Grenfell is years behind, and temporary-accommodation spending is bankrupting councils. Requires explicit dedupe rules against stagnation and civic action.
- **Research brief:** Scope housing as delivery + safety + tenure, explicitly excluding planning-system entries already in the stagnation domain and community-right-to-buy entries in civic action. Priority targets: (1) the uncommenced LAFRA 2024 provisions and the valuation-rates consultation timetable (Commons Library CBP-10653 tracks status) — a canonical 'commencement gap'; (2) commonhold transition machinery ahead of the Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Bill (2026–27 session): who trains conveyancers, lenders and managing agents, and is there a conversion support body?; (3) building safety: remediation completion gap for mid-rise buildings, no non-cladding remediation fund, Building Safety Regulator capacity; (4) missing institutions — no housing court despite repeated commitments, no land-market transparency regime (option agreements, contractual control), no at-scale acquisition vehicle for temporary-accommodation-to-social-housing conversion, and the stalled regulation of property managing agents (CBP-10918). Each entry names the fund, body or SI needed.

### 6. Science, R&D and the research system (metascience-aware)
- **Rationale:** The map is modelled on Convergent Research's gap map — whose entire subject is research-system gaps — yet has no science domain, which the founder/funder audience will immediately notice. Care is needed because some obvious asks already exist (ARIA; the UK Metascience Unit, established 2024 with ~£10m): the real gaps are one level down — no scaled channel for founding Focused Research Organisations in the UK, no replication/reproducibility infrastructure, fragile career paths for research software engineers (adjacent to but distinct from the Sovereign Tech Fund entries), punitive visa costs for researchers, and spinout terms that still lag US norms.
- **Research brief:** Inventory gaps in the UK research system that ARIA, UKRI and the Metascience Unit demonstrably do not fill. Verify first what exists: ARIA's remit, the Metascience Unit's 2026–28 'Science Systems in an AI Age' programme (metascienceunit.uk, ukri.org), and the innovation agencies' FRO-adjacent pilots. Then document: (1) no standing funding channel or legal template for UK Focused Research Organisations (compare Convergent Research's US pipeline); (2) no national replication service or negative-results infrastructure; (3) research software and data-steward maintenance careers — flag overlap with the open-source domain's Sovereign Tech Fund entry and propose which domain owns it; (4) upfront visa and health-surcharge costs for international researchers versus competitor countries; (5) the Metascience Unit's non-permanent, sub-scale status (£10m) and whether its findings have any adoption mechanism inside UKRI; (6) PhD stipend erosion and the absence of any body monitoring research-workforce supply. Rank by tractability for a private funder versus requiring government.

## Cross-cutting observations
- The map's signature pattern is 'legislated but switched off': the Statutory Debt Repayment Plan (2018), the mutuals asset-lock Act (2023), street votes, the empty debarment register, and (if housing is added) the largely uncommenced Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024. Make 'commencement gap' a first-class tag across all domains — and consider a meta-entry: no independent public tracker of uncommenced primary legislation exists in the UK, which is itself a fillable gap.
- The UK Sovereign Tech Fund appears in at least four domains (open source, portable sovereignty, funding gaps, policy gaps). The map needs canonical gap IDs with cross-references so one gap surfaces in multiple views without looking like four separate gaps — otherwise the inventory's headline count is inflated and funders may double-park on the same item.
- 'Open source tools and public goods' and 'Portable sovereignty stack' overlap heavily (Sovereign Tech Fund, encryption, sovereign messaging all straddle both). Merge them, or redraw the boundary as supply-side (maintaining public-good code) versus deployment-side (civic/state adoption of sovereign infrastructure).
- 'Surveillance' and 'Privacy and sovereignty of the individual' both contain a collective-redress gap and split encryption awkwardly with the sovereignty stack. Redraw as 'state surveillance power' versus 'commercial data rights and enforcement' — and note that every redress gap in both bottoms out in the justice system, which is why a justice domain (proposed above) is load-bearing rather than optional.
- The 'Funding gaps' and 'Policy gaps' cross-cutting domains duplicate vertical-domain entries verbatim. Convert them from domains into facets: tag every gap with its type — missing institution, missing funding instrument, uncommenced/unenforced law, missing evidence or data infrastructure, missing enforcement/redress route. That taxonomy fits nearly every sample gap listed and makes the cross-cutting views automatic rather than hand-maintained.
- 'Youth mobilisation' is a demographic lens, not a domain, and it exposes a hole: its registration and first-vote entries point at electoral administration, which no domain owns. Either fold youth into civic action, or create a 'democratic and electoral infrastructure' home covering automatic voter registration for all (not just attainers), the electoral-administrator workforce crisis, and Electoral Commission independence — which currently falls between youth and corruption.
- There is a live tension the map should surface rather than hide: the stagnation domain wants fewer regulatory drags and faster state action, while the surveillance, privacy, AI and education domains propose roughly ten new regulators, assurance bodies and certification schemes. Tag each gap with its net demand on state capacity, and note where two entries pull in opposite directions.
- Parallel-institutions formation (exit: build new co-ops, mutuals, DAOs) and corruption/integrity (voice: fix existing institutions) are complementary strategies; the map should say so explicitly, because some readers will otherwise read the parallel-institutions domain as a vote of no confidence in the reform entries.
- Coverage skew: 11 of 14 current domains are digital/governance/civic; the physical and service state — health, housing, justice, resilience, local government — is nearly absent. The six proposed additions rebalance the map toward where most UK public spending, public anxiety and founder opportunity actually sit.
- Deliberate near-misses, with suggested folds rather than new domains: energy security (split between stagnation's cost entries and the resilience domain's CNI entries); the wider media/information ecosystem (extend civic action's local-news gap; put OSA researcher-data-access in the privacy/surveillance cluster); food, farming and land use (park inside resilience until the Land Use Framework lands, then revisit as its own domain); immigration and demography (real but thinner in fillable, apolitical gap material — revisit); digital inclusion (fold into civic action as an access precondition); mental health (kept inside the proposed health domain rather than standalone).
