# Policy gaps (cross-cutting)

## Landscape
The UK's cross-cutting policy problem in mid-2026 is less absence of activity than a follow-through deficit. Parliament legislates busily — the English Devolution and Community Empowerment Act 2026 received Royal Assent in April; ECCTA's Companies House reforms are mid-rollout, with identity verification mandatory for existing directors by November 2026 — but promised frameworks elsewhere are missing or drifting. There is still no AI bill despite manifesto commitments; police live facial recognition runs on common-law powers while a Home Office consultation (closed February 2026) points to legislation roughly two years away; the Law Commission's co-operative law report lands in 2026 with no legislative slot committed; and the broad text-and-data-mining copyright exception was dropped in March 2026 with nothing legislated in its place. State capacity for evidence-based policy is fraying: the What Works Centre for Wellbeing closed in 2024 for lack of a departmental funder; only 34% of major projects have good-quality evaluation plans; civil service churn persists and the strategic workforce plan slipped repeatedly to H1 2026. Integrity machinery was reorganised — the Ethics and Integrity Commission replaced the Committee on Standards in Public Life in October 2025, ACOBA closed — but remains advisory and non-statutory, and the lobbying register still excludes in-house lobbyists. Devolution is structurally asymmetric: Scotland and Wales hold tax powers; English Strategic Authorities gained duties but almost no fiscal autonomy; Northern Ireland has minimal independent policy-analysis capacity. The recurring pattern: consultation and reorganisation substitute for statute, funding and enforcement.

## Actors
- **Institute for Government** (think tank): Whitehall Monitor tracks civil service churn and capability; Commission on the Centre of Government (2024) and calls for a civil service statute make it the lead voice on state capacity.
- **Law Commission** (statutory advisory body): Reviewing the Co-operative and Community Benefit Societies Act 2014 (final report due 2026); the chronic lag between its reports and legislation is itself a cross-cutting gap.
- **Evaluation Task Force (Cabinet Office/HMT)** (government unit): Found only 34% of Government Major Projects Portfolio projects had good-quality evaluation plans (April 2025); runs the Evaluation Registry and GMPP evaluation action plan.
- **What Works Network** (evidence network): Nine independent centres plus affiliates; the 2024 closure of the What Works Centre for Wellbeing exposed the network's fragile, department-dependent funding model.
- **Ethics and Integrity Commission** (government advisory body): Launched 13 October 2025 replacing CSPL; convenes standards bodies and reports to the PM but is explicitly not a regulator and cannot investigate cases.
- **Biometrics and Surveillance Camera Commissioner** (independent commissioner): Prof William Webster (from Nov 2025, after a year-long vacancy) is pressing for a statutory framework for police biometrics; government proposes merging his office with the Forensic Science Regulator.
- **Information Commissioner's Office** (regulator): Confirmed in December 2025 it will continue its 'public sector approach' of reprimands instead of fines, creating a public/private enforcement asymmetry.
- **Companies House** (government agency): Mid-ECCTA transformation with statutory annual progress reports (third: June 2026) and an 18 November 2026 identity-verification deadline — a rare model of tracked implementation.
- **Co-operatives UK** (trade body / network): Coordinated sector input to the Law Commission review; campaigning for a legislative vehicle to implement reform and the pledge to double the co-operative economy.
- **National Youth Agency** (charity / standards body): Interprets the s507B statutory duty for councils and documented the 73% real-terms collapse in youth service spending since 2010.
- **OpenUK** (industry body): Delivered open-source recommendations to UKRI in 2025, including a Sovereign-Tech-Fund-style maintenance vehicle; the main advocate for OSS in procurement policy.
- **Spotlight on Corruption** (charity): Documents lobbying loopholes and enforcement under-resourcing; its briefings underpin the case for extending the lobbying register to in-house lobbyists.
- **Hansard Society** (research charity): Its Delegated Legislation Review details why Parliament cannot scrutinise SIs or completed Acts systematically; key source for post-legislative scrutiny reform.
- **Pivotal Public Policy Forum** (think tank): Northern Ireland's only dedicated independent policy think tank (founded 2019); small and grant-funded — evidence of thin devolved policy-analysis capacity.
- **AI Security Institute (DSIT)** (government institute): World-leading frontier-model evaluation capability, but access agreements with labs remain voluntary and it has no statutory powers.

## Gaps
### G1. No statutory framework for police live facial recognition and biometrics
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** Live facial recognition is used by 13 of 43 forces in England and Wales (March 2026) under common-law powers, College of Policing guidance and a patchwork of data-protection and equality law. The Home Office consultation on a new legal framework closed in February 2026, with legislation expected roughly two years away — even as the Home Office funds 40 new LFR vans for national rollout. Oversight is fragmented: the BSCC post sat vacant for a year before Prof William Webster's November 2025 appointment, and the proposed BSCC/Forensic Science Regulator merger lacks statutory inspection powers. The ICO and EHRC cover parts of the ground but neither authorises deployments.
- **Why it matters:** Deployment is outpacing law: Bridges v South Wales Police (2020) already found deficiencies, and a national rollout on non-statutory foundations invites judicial reversal, inconsistent force-by-force practice, and erosion of public trust in both policing and lawful surveillance.
- **What would fill it:** A Biometrics and Surveillance Act: statutory authorisation regime with proportionality tests for LFR deployment, independent pre-authorisation, mandatory public deployment registers, and a merged biometrics/surveillance/forensics regulator with statutory inspection and audit powers. Responsible department: Home Office; strong Lords Justice and Home Affairs Committee interest.
- **Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/legal-framework-for-using-facial-recognition-in-law-enforcement ; https://post.parliament.uk/facial-recognition-technology-in-policing/ ; https://www.biometricupdate.com/202602/set-and-enforce-clear-lines-for-police-biometrics-uk-commissioner-tells-policymakers ; https://www.gov.uk/government/news/new-biometrics-and-surveillance-camera-commissioner-announced

### G2. No UK Sovereign Tech Fund paying to maintain critical open-source infrastructure
- **Type:** funding | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** Germany's Sovereign Tech Agency has invested over €24m maintaining open-source components that public and private systems depend on; the UK has no equivalent. UKRI grants fund innovation, not maintenance; Crown Commercial Service frameworks buy licences, not stewardship of community-maintained code. OpenUK delivered recommendations to UKRI in 2025 including a fund of this kind, and UK Day One (Centre for British Progress) published a costed UK Open-Source Fund proposal — but no department owns the problem and no budget line pays maintainers, despite the Technology Code of Practice requiring government to consider open source.
- **Why it matters:** Government and critical-national-infrastructure systems run on under-maintained open-source dependencies (Log4Shell-class risk). Paying maintenance is cheap sovereignty: a £20-30m/yr fund is rounding error against the cyber and resilience costs of a single major dependency failure.
- **What would fill it:** A DSIT-sponsored UK Open-Source Maintenance Fund (£20-30m/yr initially), contracting maintainers through service agreements split between proactive and reactive maintenance, modelled on Germany's Sovereign Tech Agency — hosted in UKRI digital research infrastructure or alongside NCSC for security-critical dependencies.
- **Sources:** https://britishprogress.org/uk-day-one/a-uk-open-source-fund-to-support-software-innovati ; https://openuk.uk/theuksfutureleadershipinopensource/ ; https://www.sovereign.tech/programs/fund

### G3. Co-operative law reform stranded between Law Commission report and a legislative slot
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** mid (2-7y)
- **What is missing:** The Co-operative and Community Benefit Societies Act 2014 is a consolidation of nineteenth-century industrial and provident society law. The Law Commission's review (consultation closed December 2024, ~250 responses) proposes statutory definitions of co-operatives, an overhaul of society share law, and officer duties; the final report is due in 2026. But HM Treasury has made no commitment to legislate, and Law Commission reports routinely wait years for implementation. Co-operatives UK is lobbying for a vehicle; the government's stated ambition to double the size of the co-operative and mutual sector has no legal machinery behind it.
- **Why it matters:** Outdated law is a concrete brake on the mutual economy: unclear share instruments deter capital, weak definitions invite demutualisation risk, and founders default to company structures. The reform work is already done — the gap is purely a commitment and a parliamentary slot.
- **What would fill it:** A government commitment to a Co-operatives and Community Benefit Societies Bill implementing the Law Commission's 2026 recommendations, using the special uncontroversial-Law-Commission-bill procedure in the Lords, plus resourcing for the FCA's mutuals registration function.
- **Sources:** https://lawcom.gov.uk/project/co-operatives-and-community-benefit-societies/ ; https://www.uk.coop/get-involved/law-commission

### G4. Youth services duty too weak to enforce: the s507B 'reasonably practicable' loophole
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** Section 507B Education Act 1996 obliges English councils to secure 'sufficient' youth activities only 'so far as reasonably practicable' — a qualifier that let spending fall 73% (2010/11-2022/23) with 1,000+ youth centre closures and no successful enforcement. The December 2025 National Youth Strategy ('Youth Matters', £500m over three years, 250 refurbished facilities, 50 Young Futures Hubs) commits only to 'explore reviewing' the duty within a wider review of local statutory duties. The National Youth Agency issues guidance but has no lever; the £8m Local Youth Transformation pilot ends August 2026 with no successor mechanism confirmed.
- **Why it matters:** Non-statutory services are cut first in every fiscal squeeze; the strategy's own 2035 ambitions (500,000 more young people with a trusted adult) are undeliverable if the underlying duty stays unenforceable. This is the clearest live example of a duty existing on paper without teeth.
- **What would fill it:** An amended s507B with a defined sufficiency standard and needs-assessment duty, a ringfenced or formula-based funding line, and monitoring/inspection (DCMS-DfE joint ownership). The promised statutory-duty review is the near-term vehicle; DCMS is the responsible department.
- **Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/youth-matters-your-national-youth-strategy/youth-matters-your-national-youth-strategy ; https://www.cypnow.co.uk/content/news/government-commits-to-review-council-youth-work-duty-in-500mn-national-strategy ; https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10132/ ; https://nya.org.uk/support-for-local-authorities/statutory-duty/

### G5. No resilient core-funding mechanism for What Works Centres and evidence synthesis
- **Type:** institutional | **Horizon:** mid (2-7y)
- **What is missing:** The What Works Centre for Wellbeing closed in April 2024 because, unlike sibling centres, it had no single departmental funder — cross-cutting evidence had no owner, and named peers publicly blamed 'lack of appetite from funders'. The remaining nine-centre network depends on ad hoc departmental and charitable grants; centres are born and die by funder fashion (UKRI is now tendering a new What Works Centre for local employment support) rather than by evidence need. No standing facility guarantees continuity for accredited evidence institutions or owns topics that span departments.
- **Why it matters:** Evidence infrastructure is a public good with exactly the funding-timescale mismatch the map should flag: synthesis pays off over decades, grants run three years. Losing a centre destroys curated evidence, networks and institutional memory that cannot be quickly rebuilt.
- **What would fill it:** A cross-government evidence-endowment or statutory core-funding facility (administered by the Evaluation Task Force or UKRI) providing 10-year accreditation-based core grants to What Works Centres, with explicit provision for cross-departmental topics such as wellbeing that lack a home department.
- **Sources:** https://whatworkswellbeing.org/blog/statement-of-closure/ ; https://www.gov.uk/guidance/what-works-network ; https://www.ukri.org/opportunity/what-works-centre-wwc-for-local-employment-support/

### G6. No binding duty to evaluate major projects — and no systematic post-legislative scrutiny
- **Type:** knowledge | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** The Evaluation Task Force's April 2025 review found only 34% of Government Major Projects Portfolio projects had good-quality evaluation plans, leaving £456bn of spend effectively unevaluated; its action plan (due complete March 2026) relies on process change, not obligation. In Parliament, departments are supposed to submit post-legislative memoranda 3-5 years after Royal Assent, but follow-up is sporadic and the 2012 Lords recommendation for a standing Post-Legislative Scrutiny Committee was never implemented. The Hansard Society documents the same weakness for delegated legislation. Result: neither spend nor statute is systematically checked against intended effect.
- **Why it matters:** Government cannot learn what works if evaluation is optional: two-thirds of major-project spend has no credible feedback loop, and Acts like the Lobbying Act only got scrutinised by committee exception. This gap silently compounds every other gap on the map.
- **What would fill it:** Make a robust, published evaluation plan a condition of HM Treasury business-case approval (Green Book/NISTA gate) with results logged in the public Evaluation Registry; and establish a standing House of Lords post-legislative scrutiny committee with a rolling programme of Act reviews.
- **Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/progress-report-on-the-government-major-projects-evaluation-review-action-plan/progress-report-on-the-government-major-projects-evaluation-review-action-plan-html ; https://erskinemay.parliament.uk/section/4989/postlegislative-scrutiny-of-acts/ ; https://www.hansardsociety.org.uk/publications/briefings/online-safety-delegated-legislation-social-media-ai

### G7. No strategic workforce plan for the civil service; churn remains structural
- **Type:** institutional | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** IfG's Whitehall Monitor 2026 finds turnover falling only because of recruitment freezes, not retention: officials still must change jobs to win promotion or a pay rise, and churn costs an estimated £73m/yr in recruitment and training alone. The long-promised strategic workforce plan slipped from summer 2025 to end-2025 to 'first half of 2026'. Fixes exist in pockets — the digital profession has in-role pay progression, the FCDO uses minimum service terms and records among the lowest turnover — but no department-wide mechanism, and no one is accountable for turnover levels.
- **Why it matters:** Churn destroys institutional memory and negotiating capability precisely where policy is hardest (tax, procurement, infrastructure). Every other implementation gap on this map is partly downstream of officials rotating every 18-24 months.
- **What would fill it:** Publication and enforcement of the strategic workforce plan: turnover targets owned by permanent secretaries, in-role pay progression extended across professions, minimum tenure expectations for senior policy roles — plus the IfG-proposed civil service statute to entrench workforce accountability.
- **Sources:** https://www.instituteforgovernment.org.uk/publication/whitehall-monitor-2026/part-2-state-civil-service ; https://www.peoplemanagement.co.uk/article/1742799/employee-churn-costs-civil-service-73-million-per-year ; https://www.instituteforgovernment.org.uk/publication/power-with-purpose-centre-commission

### G8. Frontier AI oversight rests on voluntary agreements; the promised AI bill is unlegislated
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** As of mid-2026 no AI bill has been introduced, despite the 2024 manifesto commitment to binding rules on the most powerful models; the legislative timetable has slipped repeatedly. The AI Security Institute's pre-deployment model access and evaluations remain voluntary arrangements with labs, revocable at will. On copyright, the government's March 2026 statement of progress under s137 of the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 confirmed the broad text-and-data-mining exception is dropped with no replacement legislated — leaving both AI developers and creative industries in legal limbo. Sector regulators (via DRCF) apply existing law at point of use but lack AI-specific capacity funding.
- **Why it matters:** Voluntary access can be withdrawn exactly when it matters most, and the UK's credibility as the home of frontier-AI evaluation depends on AISI's standing. Meanwhile the unresolved training-data regime chills both AI investment and creative-sector licensing revenue.
- **What would fill it:** A targeted frontier AI statute: statutory footing for AISI pre-deployment access, mandatory safety-incident reporting and transparency duties for frontier developers; plus a legislated transparency-and-licensing settlement for AI training data. Responsible department: DSIT; active Commons Science, Innovation and Technology Committee interest.
- **Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/copyright-and-artificial-intelligence-progress-report/copyright-and-artificial-intelligence-statement-of-progress-under-section-137-data-use-and-access-act ; https://www.pinsentmasons.com/out-law/analysis/ai-and-copyright-post-data-bill-uk-timeline-2026 ; https://www.longtermresilience.org/reports/advancing-the-uks-global-leadership-in-frontier-ai-governance/ ; https://www.aisi.gov.uk/

### G9. English devolution built structures without money: no fiscal powers for Strategic Authorities
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** mid (2-7y)
- **What is missing:** The English Devolution and Community Empowerment Act 2026 (Royal Assent 29 April 2026) creates Strategic Authorities, expands mayoral powers and reforms mayoral elections — but revenue powers remain limited to council-tax precepts and a promised visitor levy. Integrated settlements (Greater Manchester and West Midlands from 2025-26; North East, South/West Yorkshire, Liverpool City Region and GLA from 2026-27) are spending flexibilities hedged with conditions and reporting, not fiscal autonomy. Scotland and Wales set income-tax rates; English mayors cannot. Re:State, LabourList commentators and mayors themselves now argue tax assignment is the missing piece; the Treasury has conceded only 'consideration' of a business-rates share.
- **Why it matters:** Structures without revenue reproduce the begging-bowl model devolution was meant to end: mayors accountable for growth cannot borrow or invest against their own tax base, blunting the Act's entire growth rationale and keeping England the most fiscally centralised large economy in the OECD.
- **What would fill it:** A second-stage fiscal devolution framework: assigned shares of income tax or business rates for established Strategic Authorities, multi-year condition-light settlements, and borrowing flexibility with independent local fiscal scrutiny. Responsible: HM Treasury and MHCLG; NAO already tracking.
- **Sources:** https://bills.parliament.uk/bills/4002 ; https://www.gov.uk/government/news/english-devolution-bill-receives-royal-assent ; https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/explanatory-note-on-mayoral-strategic-authority-funding-2026-to-2027-final/explanatory-note-on-mayoral-strategic-authority-funding-2026-to-2027-final ; https://www.room151.co.uk/funding/mayoral-strategic-authorities-need-tax-powers-instead-of-integrated-settlements-argues-think-tank/

### G10. The statutory lobbying register still excludes in-house lobbyists
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** The Transparency of Lobbying Act 2014 registers only consultant lobbyists communicating with ministers and permanent secretaries — in-house lobbyists, the large majority, are invisible, and lobbying of special advisers escapes the net entirely. PACAC's post-legislative scrutiny recommended expansion; Spotlight on Corruption documents the continuing loopholes; a Lobbying Transparency (In-house Lobbyists) Bill [HL] is before Parliament in 2026 and a government review touching lobbying is due to report before summer recess 2026. Adjacent opacity compounds it: nine of 28 think tanks scored 'E' on openDemocracy's Who Funds You, holding £14.3m of untraceable income. The new Ethics and Integrity Commission is advisory only.
- **Why it matters:** Policy influence is systematically undocumented: the register captures a sliver of actual lobbying while think-tank 'dark money' shapes debate unattributed. This corrodes trust in every other policy process and is cheap to fix relative to its integrity payoff.
- **What would fill it:** Amend the 2014 Act: extend the register to in-house lobbying above an activity threshold, widen 'communications' to include special advisers and senior officials, and add funding-transparency disclosure duties for organisations engaged in policy influence. Responsible: Cabinet Office; live private-member's-bill vehicle exists.
- **Sources:** https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm5804/cmselect/cmpubadm/203/report.html ; https://www.spotlightcorruption.org/report/briefing-lobbying-loopholes/ ; https://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/LLN-2026-0034/LLN-2026-0034.pdf ; https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/dark-money-investigations/think-tanks-transparency-funding-who-funds-you/

### G11. No independent costing service for opposition and backbench policy
- **Type:** institutional | **Horizon:** mid (2-7y)
- **What is missing:** The Budget Responsibility and National Audit Act 2011 restricts the OBR to costing government policy only, so opposition parties enter elections with uncosted or self-costed platforms. Canada's Parliamentary Budget Office costs opposition policies confidentially year-round; the Netherlands' CPB scores full manifestos a month before polling day. The IFS partially fills the gap at elections but lacks access to departmental models and cannot certify. IfG analysis supports OBR manifesto costing while noting resourcing and impartiality design questions; Labour previously advocated it in opposition but has not legislated in government.
- **Why it matters:** Uncosted platforms degrade electoral debate and produce governments locked into undeliverable promises — a root cause of the announce-then-unravel cycle. Independent costing raises the quality of opposition policy years before it becomes government policy.
- **What would fill it:** Amend the 2011 Act to permit confidential OBR costing of registered parties' policies in a defined pre-election window, with published certifications — or create a small Parliamentary Budget Office serving all parties, on the Canadian model. Responsible: HM Treasury; Treasury Committee interest.
- **Sources:** https://www.instituteforgovernment.org.uk/article/comment/asking-obr-cost-manifestos-could-make-sense-would-be-complicated ; https://obr.uk/faqs/ ; https://www.bennettschool.cam.ac.uk/blog/wheres-money-coming-tracing-history-manifesto-cost/

### G12. Data protection law is effectively unenforced against the public sector
- **Type:** policy | **Horizon:** short (0-2y)
- **What is missing:** The ICO's 'public sector approach' — reconfirmed in December 2025 — substitutes reprimands for fines: six of seven UK GDPR reprimands in 2025 went to public bodies, and the Post Office data breach drew a reprimand where a £1.09m fine had been calculated. Analysis suggests fines under normal rules would have totalled £23.2m against an actual £1.2m. The ICO's rationale (fines just shuffle public money) has force, but no alternative sanction with equivalent deterrence has been created, and the regulator's own resourcing constrains oversight of surveillance-adjacent grey areas it inherited.
- **Why it matters:** Citizens have no meaningful remedy when the state mishandles their data, and public bodies face weaker incentives than private firms for identical failures — exactly as government expands data sharing, biometrics and AI use. An implementation gap in the strictest sense: law exists, sanction does not.
- **What would fill it:** A statutory alternative-sanctions regime for public bodies: enforceable remediation orders with a named accountable senior officer, mandatory ICO follow-up audits, accounting-officer consequences for non-compliance, and annual parliamentary reporting — designed with DSIT as part of post-DUAA regulatory review.
- **Sources:** https://actnowtraining.blog/2025/12/02/ico-public-sector-enforcement-policy-to-continue/ ; https://www.urmconsulting.com/blog/analysis-of-enforcement-action-by-the-ico-in-2025-actions-way-down-security-data-breach-fines-way-up ; https://measuredcollective.com/icos-public-sector-approach-why-the-post-office-received-a-reprimand-instead-of-a-1m-fine/

## Policy notes
Think-tank coverage is dense on macro-fiscal policy (IFS, Resolution Foundation, NIESR) and Westminster machinery (IfG, Hansard Society) but thin on implementation and delivery, local government finance, and the devolved nations: Pivotal is Northern Ireland's only dedicated policy think tank and is small; Wales leans on the university-based Wales Centre for Public Policy. Funding opacity is itself a policy problem — nine of 28 major think tanks rate 'E' on Who Funds You. For the map, each policy-gap entry should record: responsible department(s); current instrument and bill status (including private members' bills); consultation or Law Commission stage with deadlines; select committee interest; commencement and enforcement status where law already exists (the Companies House statutory progress reports are the model); enforcement body and its resourcing; cost estimate or fiscal note; devolved applicability (England-only vs UK-wide); named champions; and a review-by date, since bill status decays fast.
